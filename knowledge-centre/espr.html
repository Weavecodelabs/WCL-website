Understanding the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR)

The European Union's New Rulebook for Sustainable Products

Category

Regulations

Reading Time

8 Minutes

Last Updated

July 2026

Author

WeaveCode Labs Editorial Team

The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) is the European Union's flagship legislation designed to make products sold in the EU more sustainable, circular and transparent throughout their entire lifecycle.

Adopted in 2024, ESPR replaces the previous Ecodesign Directive, which primarily focused on energy-related products. The new regulation significantly expands its scope, covering nearly all physical products placed on the EU market over time, including textiles, furniture, electronics, batteries, steel, chemicals and many other product categories.

Its objective is simple: design products that last longer, consume fewer resources, are easier to repair and recycle, and provide transparent sustainability information to consumers and regulators.

Why Was ESPR Introduced?

The European Green Deal aims to make Europe climate-neutral by 2050. Achieving this goal requires products to become more sustainable from the design stage itself rather than only at the end of their lifecycle.

Today, many products are difficult to repair, heavily dependent on virgin materials, poorly documented and challenging to recycle. Consumers and regulators often have limited access to reliable sustainability information.

ESPR addresses these challenges by introducing mandatory ecodesign requirements and improving transparency across global supply chains.

Common Challenges Today

  • Difficult to repair.
  • Made primarily from virgin materials.
  • Poorly documented product information.
  • Limited recyclability.
  • Lack of trusted sustainability data.

Key Objectives of ESPR

ESPR establishes a common framework for improving product sustainability throughout the European market. The regulation encourages businesses to design products that are more durable, resource-efficient and suitable for a circular economy.

  • Improve product durability and reliability.
  • Increase repairability and upgradeability.
  • Promote reuse and remanufacturing.
  • Increase recycled content.
  • Improve recyclability at end-of-life.
  • Reduce environmental and carbon impacts.
  • Improve resource efficiency.
  • Increase transparency across supply chains.

Digital Product Passport (DPP)

One of the most significant requirements introduced under ESPR is the Digital Product Passport (DPP).

A Digital Product Passport is a structured digital record that stores important information about a product throughout its lifecycle. It enables consumers, businesses, recyclers and authorities to access reliable product information through a QR code or another approved data carrier.

A Digital Product Passport may include:

  • Product Identification
  • Material Composition
  • Manufacturing Details
  • Supply Chain Traceability
  • Environmental Performance
  • Chemicals of Concern
  • Care & Repair Information
  • Recycling & End-of-Life Guidance
  • Compliance Documentation

The Digital Product Passport is expected to become one of the key tools supporting transparency and sustainability across multiple product sectors under ESPR.

Which Products Are Covered?

The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) is a framework regulation. Instead of applying identical requirements to every product immediately, the European Commission will introduce product-specific Delegated Acts over the coming years.

Each Delegated Act will define the ecodesign requirements, Digital Product Passport obligations and information requirements for a particular product category.

Priority Product Sectors

  • Textiles & Apparel
  • Furniture
  • Iron & Steel
  • Aluminium
  • Electronics
  • ICT Products
  • Tyres
  • Chemicals
  • Energy-related Products

Additional product groups are expected to be introduced gradually as the regulation expands across industries.

What Does This Mean for Textile Businesses?

For textile manufacturers, exporters, brands and suppliers selling products into the European Union, ESPR represents a significant shift in how product information is collected, managed and shared.

Rather than relying on disconnected spreadsheets and paper documentation, businesses will increasingly need structured digital product information that can be maintained throughout the product lifecycle.

Businesses Should Begin Preparing By

  • Collecting accurate product information.
  • Improving supply chain traceability.
  • Documenting material origins.
  • Measuring sustainability metrics.
  • Preparing for Digital Product Passports.
  • Maintaining digital product records.
  • Improving collaboration with suppliers.

Companies that digitise their product data early will be far better positioned to comply with future regulatory requirements while improving operational efficiency.

Benefits Beyond Compliance

Although ESPR introduces new regulatory responsibilities, it also creates opportunities for businesses to improve competitiveness, transparency and customer trust.

Organisations that invest in better product information and digital systems often gain advantages that extend well beyond regulatory compliance.

Potential Business Benefits

  • Greater customer trust.
  • Improved supply chain visibility.
  • Higher operational efficiency.
  • Simplified sustainability reporting.
  • Enhanced brand credibility.
  • Support for circular economy initiatives.
  • Improved competitiveness in international markets.

Businesses that embrace digital transformation today are more likely to adapt successfully as sustainability expectations continue to evolve.

The Road Ahead

The European Commission will continue publishing Delegated Acts that define the detailed requirements for individual product categories under ESPR.

Alongside these requirements, supporting infrastructure— including the EU Digital Product Passport Registry, European technical standards and interoperability frameworks—is being developed to enable secure and consistent exchange of product information across industries.

While implementation will occur over several years, businesses should not wait until requirements become mandatory. Early preparation will reduce compliance challenges and create a stronger foundation for digital product management.

Conclusion

The Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) represents one of the most significant policy changes for product sustainability in recent decades. Rather than focusing solely on how products are manufactured, ESPR considers their entire lifecycle—from design and material selection to repair, reuse, recycling and end-of-life management.

At the centre of this transformation is the Digital Product Passport (DPP), which enables trusted product information to be shared securely between manufacturers, suppliers, consumers, recyclers and regulators.

Although implementation will take place gradually through product-specific Delegated Acts, businesses should begin preparing now by improving product data management, supply chain traceability and digital infrastructure.

Organisations that embrace these changes early will not only be better prepared for compliance but will also strengthen customer trust, improve operational efficiency and position themselves for success in an increasingly transparent and circular global economy.
